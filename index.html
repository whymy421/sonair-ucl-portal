About the Lab

Robotics & Teleoperation

The UCL Robotics Lab focuses on remote teleoperation, sim2real transfer, and networked robot control. We investigate how network latency and jitter affect the fidelity of commands transmitted between simulation environments and physical hardware.

We are open to collaboration on federated benchmarking, cross-institutional teleoperation experiments, and shared dataset curation for the SONAIR network.

Interpretation

Network stability. Mean RTT of ~55 ms with a range of 14–401 ms indicates generally responsive conditions, but with significant outlier spikes. The P95 value shows 5% of measurements exceed a much higher threshold, suggesting intermittent congestion on the UCL–Nottingham link.

Command latency. The histogram clusters tightly around 20–40 ms — acceptable for non-safety-critical teleoperation. A long tail beyond 100 ms means some commands experience delays that could cause visible desynchronisation between operator input and robot response.

Sim2real implication. In simulation, commands execute instantaneously. In reality, variable latency introduces a timing gap. A trajectory planned at 50 Hz assumes each command arrives within 20 ms, but the network delivers with ~25 ms standard deviation. The physical robot diverges from the simulated trajectory, particularly during fast motions.

Session events. The audit log shows a burst of link_degraded rejections near t=420 s, correlating with RTT spikes. Network instability directly impacts teleoperation reliability.

Motion Interpretation

Trajectory pattern. The TCP trajectory shows the robot operated within a compact workspace, with the end-effector moving primarily in the XY plane. Clusters indicate repeated positioning tasks or fine adjustments during teleoperation.

Speed profile. Speed varies significantly — periods of near-zero speed alternate with faster movements. Speed spikes reveal rapid repositioning commands, the most latency-sensitive phases for sim2real fidelity.

Joint coordination. Joint angles show coordinated multi-joint motion, confirming active manipulation. The joint data captures the robot's configuration space, complementing the Cartesian TCP view.

Sim2real relevance. Combined with RTT data, motion data reveals which movements coincide with latency spikes. High-speed motions during high-latency periods represent the worst-case sim2real gap.

Challenge 1

Sim2Real Gap Metric — Temporal Fidelity Index

A novel metric to quantify the simulation-to-real timing gap for networked teleoperation.

We propose the Temporal Fidelity Index (TFI) — a composite metric that captures how faithfully a real networked teleoperation system reproduces the timing assumptions of its simulation counterpart. Existing sim2real metrics focus on positional or visual accuracy; TFI targets the temporal dimension.

Definition

TFI = (1 − Dnorm) × (1 − Jnorm) × (1 − Lrate)
  • Dnorm — normalised mean excess delay = mean(RTT) / max(RTT).
  • Jnorm — normalised jitter = std(RTT) / max(RTT).
  • Lrate — packet loss rate = lost / total.

Why It Matters

For teleoperation, timing fidelity is often more critical than positional accuracy — delayed commands accumulate into trajectory divergence. A trajectory planned at 50 Hz assumes each command arrives within 20 ms, but the real network introduces variable delays that compound over time.

Adoption Across SONAIR

TFI can be computed for any teleoperation session that logs RTT and packet loss. Each campus node could publish its TFI alongside raw data, enabling cross-institutional comparison and establishing minimum fidelity thresholds for safety-critical applications.
